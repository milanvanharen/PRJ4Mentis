Hierarchy

Users have limited time and attention when viewing our website. That’s why we put certain elements in a certain order. For example, on the home page the users first get greeted with a big background picture and an inspiring quote, after he immediately sees “At Mentis, we can help you with:”. This should make him/her interested. When further scrolling down, the users can see our team, some testimonials, and – very important – a notice to donate and keep out platform alive. On every block, the user can find a button to find out more on the dedicated page. So if the users see our team, he/she can click a button to get to the team page.

Navigation Menu

The navigation menu is a very important part of our website. We have therefore also tried to use it to our advantage: The menu bar has a dark color as standard, but when the user scrolls up it becomes transparent (not on mobile! On mobile, the bar always stays dark), So that the design blends in nicely with the image in the header. Is the text in the menu bar difficult to read? No problem! Scroll your mouse over the menu bar and it will go dark again. This dark color also colors extra well with the blue gradient of the scroll progress indicator.

In the menu bar itself, some important items, such as Donate and Join Mentis, are colored with a button, so that they stand out. We, therefore, hope that the user will click these buttons more quickly.

Pictures

Every page has its own prominently placed picture. Simultaneously, a picture says a thousand words and it keeps a calm atmosphere. All the images we used are there for a reason. Either to explain something or to add to the visual aesthetic of the website. Next to the pictures we also made use of drawn images to create a friendly appearance.

Colors

We used two main colors in our website. Grey (off black and light grey), and Blue (Deep blue and light blue). The reason for this choice is that we wanted to work with colorful pictures. If we would have used more colors in our websites’ palet, the contrast between text and image would have been too small. Therefore, we decided to use our blue brand color and an off black grayish tint. Because we continued this level of contrast through all the pages, everything is clear and legible. Important content has a bigger contrast with the background opposed to less important things which have a smaller contrast. This way the readability is off high standard and the user can easily differentiate the important from the less important.

Usability Heuristics

Visibility of system status

On the top of the page, the user sees a scrolling bar that fills up as the user scrolls down on the page. This keeps the user informed about what is going on on the page.

Match between system and real world

We do not like reading complicated words that we do not understand as for we did not apply any in our own website. Our site is written in a normal-people-language that everyone can understand.

Error prevention and helping users recognize, diagnose, and recover from errors

Errors are not fun. For nobody. That is why both our site's contact form and sign up/log in pages have built-in error prevention. An example of this is trying to fill in an email adress without using a @. The site will notice this and the user will get a warning message saying that the email adress might be invalid. To improve the user friendlyness even more, we created a scroll-to-top button.

Aesthetic and minimalistic design

Our website uses a minimalistic design by only using a few colors. This fact combined with the prominently displayed pictures makes the site aesthetically pleasing and does not distract the users' attention from what is important.

User testing and improvement suggestions

For one of the assignments relating to PRJ4, namely the Lean Canvas, we had to create a paper prototype of a solution to a problem. As we are a mental health nonprofit organization, our first instinct was to create a page with testimonials and people sharing their experiences. The image above is what we came up with, and what we shared with people. The feedback we got was quite clear. For the testimonials, people would like to see variations. Not just written text, but also videos for example. They also mentioned that this was a very busy page for a home page. There was a lot going on, and simpler images were nicer to see and made it a bit more relaxed. We try to achieve tranquility and a safe space, so knowing that people found that there was too much going on on one page made sense. They pointed out that a home page with multiple parts is not bad as it is the home page and people want to instantly know what’s going on. However, the testimonials page should be kept separate and clear.

The second done was actual user testing. The image above shows a sketch of what the first draft of our actual website looked like. We let a few people scroll through the website and figure out what they thought. Unfortunately, the home page was still too busy. People pointed out that the images were too small, so we made those bigger. The donation button was way below on the home page, and people hardly noticed it was there as they didn’t always instantly scroll down. Therefore, we decided to add this button noticeably in the navbar. The font in the navbar was big and therefore hard to read and navigate, a few users pointed out. We decided to make the font a bit smaller so it was easier to instantly see all the buttons. Pages that were missed and took too much effort to get to, were also added to the navbar, as users wanted to be able to intstantly without any scrolling see what pages they have access to.
